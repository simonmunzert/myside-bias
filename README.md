# Replication materials for: Myside Bias Undermines Online Content Moderation

## Abstract

Online platforms rely on humans and AI to moderate toxic speech, yet AI systems typically inherit the biases embedded in the human labels they are trained on. Drawing on an original cross-national visual conjoint experiment including 19k respondents in 11 countries, we analyze over 150k human judgments of toxic social media posts. Across contexts, people support keeping non-threatening speech online, but views on what counts as offensive or hateful diverge sharply. Strikingly, we can show that moderation decisions are heavily shaped by the rater’s stance on the issue at hand. This myside bias transcends ideology, topic, speech type, and geography. These results reveal a fundamental challenge for content moderation: when perceptions of harm and support for sanctioning are rooted in subjective viewpoints, no technical fix can yield universally legitimate enforcement. This calls for rethinking the design and legitimacy of automated enforcement in polarized environments.
