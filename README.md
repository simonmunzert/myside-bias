# Replication materials for: Myside Bias Shapes Preferences for Online Content Moderation
## Abstract

Online content moderation shapes what billions of people can say and see. Yet the human judgments that underpin it-how people perceive toxic speech and what they want done in response-diverge sharply. We argue that a key source of this divergence is myside bias: a self-serving tendency to judge speech by whether one agrees with the position it targets. Drawing on a pre-registered conjoint experiment with 19,172 respondents across 11 countries, we analyze over 150,000 lay judgments of toxic social media posts. We find robust evidence of myside bias that holds across countries, political camps, topics, and speech types, and that persists when controlling for general ideological alignment. Because the bias is in the judgments themselves and can shift issue by issue, it cannot easily be engineered away. Myside bias thus poses a structural challenge for content governance in online environments.
